The scenario list below is re-ranked based on a combination of official March 2026 Bank of Canada language and structured news searches across candidate themes.

## Interpretation & Caveats

- The site uses textbook diagrams as teaching benchmarks, not as fitted empirical models of Canada in March 2026.
- Section 2 uses a classic money-market representation. Operationally, the Bank of Canada implements policy through its rate corridor and expectations channel rather than a literal money-stock target.
- Section 3 holds commodity prices, terms of trade and risk premia fixed, so parity should be read as a benchmark rather than a spot-rate equation.
- Section 4 uses headline CPI as a rough long-run input. The February 2026 Canadian print is not a clean valuation signal because of tax-related distortions.
- Section 5 compresses goods, services, primary income and transfers into a simplified current-account proxy; it should not be read as a claim that one slider reproduces the official BOP.
- Section 9 is best read as a funding-and-liquidity stress exercise, not as a claim that Canada is facing a classic reserve-defense currency crisis.

### 1. US Tariffs / Trade Policy Uncertainty
- The March 18, 2026 Bank of Canada statement explicitly says Canada is adjusting to **US tariffs and trade policy uncertainty**
- This is the clearest downside scenario for Canadian growth because it directly hits exports, business confidence and investment
- In the FX framework: weaker exports reduce USD inflows to Canada and can put downward pressure on CAD
- **Net effect**: negative for Canadian growth; typically negative for CAD unless offset by another shock

### 2. Middle East War / Energy Shock
- The Bank of Canada says the war in the Middle East has increased volatility in global energy prices and financial markets
- Higher oil and gas prices can support Canada's terms of trade and export income
- But the same shock raises inflation risk, tightens financial conditions and can strengthen safe-haven USD demand
- **Net effect**: mixed for CAD and clearly uncomfortable for inflation

### 3. Household Debt / Mortgage Renewal Strain
- Bank of Canada financial stability material continues to emphasize debt-service stress, vulnerable mortgage cohorts and household repayment capacity
- Weak housing conditions and soft labour markets make this especially relevant for 2026
- This is less dramatic than a crisis narrative, but more directly relevant to many Canadian households
- **Net effect**: downside risk to domestic demand; increases pressure on the BoC if growth weakens further

### 4. Non-Bank / Private Credit Repricing
- Current Canadian reporting still points to severe-repricing risk in non-bank finance and private credit
- The Bank of Canada's financial system survey highlights scenarios that could trigger a severe repricing in financial markets in the near term
- For Canada, the main channel is tighter funding, wider spreads and weaker risk appetite rather than an immediate base-case crisis
- **Net effect**: downside tail risk for CAD, credit conditions and pension portfolios

### 5. LNG / Export Diversification Upside
- Structured searches increasingly surface Canada's LNG build-out as a strategic medium-term theme
- This matters more for the next several years than for the exact March 2026 spot rate
- Diversifying exports toward Asia and potentially Europe would improve Canada's terms of trade resilience and reduce sole dependence on the US market at the margin
- **Net effect**: medium-term upside for trade, investment and CAD resilience, not a short-run baseline shock

## Section-by-Section Mapping

### Section 1: FX Market (Supply & Demand for USD)
- **Y-axis**: Exchange rate E = CAD per USD (higher = weaker CAD)
- **Demand for USD**: Canadians buying US goods, investing in US assets, paying USD debts
- **Supply of USD**: Americans buying Canadian goods, investing in Canada, buying oil
- **Realistic defaults**: E ≈ 1.38–1.39 CAD/USD
- **Try this**: Middle East energy shock → supply shifts right, but safe-haven USD demand can also rise → mixed CAD outcome
- **Try this**: Tariff threat → supply shifts left and demand edges right → CAD weakens
- **Try this**: export or terms-of-trade upside → supply shifts right over time → medium-term CAD support
- Avoid using non-US export diversification as a literal USD-flow example inside the bilateral CAD/USD diagram

### Section 2: Money → Interest → FX
- **Mˢ slider** = stylized BoC policy stance / money-supply proxy
- **i* (foreign rate)** = Fed funds rate ≈ 3.6%
- **Eᵉ (expected future)** = where markets think CAD/USD is heading
- **BoC easing**: more money → lower Canadian rates → capital flows to US → CAD weakens
- **Current reality**: BoC at 2.25% vs Fed at 3.50–3.75% is one important reason CAD is trading around 1.38–1.39
- **Scenario links**: weak growth and household strain push toward easier BoC policy; tariffs and risk-off can weaken Eᵉ; Fed easing narrows the gap and helps CAD
- Operational caveat: the real BoC framework is a policy-rate corridor, not a literal quantity target for money

### Section 3: Interest Rate Parity
- **Home rate** = BoC rate ≈ 2.25%
- **Foreign rate** = Fed rate ≈ 3.6%
- UIP says: CAD can be weak NOW while still being expected to strengthen later (expected CAD appreciation compensates for lower rates in the benchmark model)
- CIP: forward CAD/USD should be below spot (forward discount on USD / premium on CAD)
- Current 1Y forward: ~1.38 vs spot ~1.39 → consistent with CIP
- **Caveat**: use parity as a benchmark, not as a literal one-equation forecast of spot FX
- Missing wedge: commodity prices, safe-haven USD demand and risk premia can all move spot away from the parity benchmark

### Section 4: PPP
- **Canada inflation** ≈ 1.8%, **US inflation** ≈ 2.4%
- PPP says: lower Canadian inflation → CAD should slowly appreciate in real terms
- BUT the February 2026 Canadian headline print is flattered by tax effects, so the signal should be treated cautiously
- **Starting E** = about 1.38–1.39
- Better use case: PPP as a slow long-run anchor, not a short-run timing signal

### Section 5: Balance of Payments
- Canada is not running a large rolling 2% GDP current-account deficit in the latest official quarter; the Q4 2025 deficit narrowed sharply to C$0.7B even though 2025 as a whole still posted a C$30.4B deficit
- The site starts with a modest external shortfall because the section is teaching the accounting logic, not reproducing one quarter exactly
- Energy exports partially offset goods import deficit
- **J-curve relevant**: if CAD depreciates 10%, Canadian trade balance can worsen first (import prices spike), then improve if quantities adjust enough
- **Scenario links**: tariffs widen the trade gap; energy shocks improve terms of trade; the rate gap changes how comfortable external financing feels

### Section 6: Mundell-Fleming
- Canada = **FLOATING rate** + **free capital** → monetary policy has more short-run traction in the textbook case
- **BoC rate cut** = monetary expansion → LM shifts right → lower rates → capital outflows → CAD depreciates → exports rise → IS shifts right → output increases in the textbook setup
- **Fiscal expansion** (deficit spending): under floating, appreciation offsets part of the stimulus in the textbook setup, but that does not mean fiscal policy is irrelevant in practice
- Compare to Eurozone members (fixed within euro, free capital): fiscal policy is more potent there
- **Current lens**: tariffs are a negative IS shock; BoC easing partly cushions that shock through the exchange rate

### Section 7: Overshooting
- **BoC surprise cut**: if BoC cuts more than expected, CAD overshoots — drops past its new long-run level, then gradually appreciates back
- Canadian prices are sticky (wages, rents, contracts denominated in CAD)
- This explains why CAD/USD moves 2-3% on rate announcement days but then drifts back
- **Non-bank funding shock**: sudden liquidity shock → larger stylized overshoot as exchange rate absorbs the blow while prices haven't adjusted
- **Caveat**: the chart is illustrative, not a fitted model of a specific historical episode

### Section 8: Trilemma
- **Canada chose**: floating rate + free capital + monetary independence ← the "Free Capital + Policy Autonomy" option
- Sacrificed: fixed exchange rate (CAD floats freely)
- This is why BoC CAN cut when Fed holds — but the cost is a weaker CAD
- **If Canada tried to peg** to USD: would need to match Fed rates → couldn't cut to help housing market → economy would struggle
- **Current lens**: floating helps Canada absorb tariff shocks, energy shocks, and funding stress without abandoning monetary independence

### Section 9: Crisis
- **Moderate vulnerability**: funding-market stress, non-bank repricing and household weakness matter more than a classic emerging-market currency crisis story
- **Strong factors**: credible liquidity backstops, AAA sovereign credit, prudent regulation and Fed swap-line access
- **Scenario**: if private credit unwind triggers margin calls on Canadian institutions → sudden USD demand → CAD drops → net FX liability burden rises → feedback loop
- **Mitigant**: BoC USD swap line with Fed, adequate reserves, credible policy framework
- Not crisis-like now, but the ingredients for market stress are present if trade shocks and funding shocks arrive together
