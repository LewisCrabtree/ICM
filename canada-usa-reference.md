## Current Macro Shocks (as of March 2026)

### 1. Iran War / Middle East Escalation
- **Oil price spike**: Brent pushed above $90+, supporting CAD via energy exports
- Canada is a major oil exporter (oil sands); US is now also a major producer but net importer
- War risk premium → higher oil → shifts USD supply curve RIGHT (more USD flowing to Canada for oil)
- Also: global risk-off → USD safe haven demand → partially offsets CAD strength
- **Net effect**: mixed for CAD; oil helps, but risk-off flight-to-USD hurts

### 2. AI Bubble / Tech Correction
- Massive US tech valuations starting to wobble; Nasdaq correction
- Previous capital flows INTO US tech → those reverse → USD supply increases, USD demand decreases
- Canada also exposed: Toronto tech sector, pension funds heavily invested in US tech
- If US tech wealth evaporates → less US spending → less Canadian exports
- **Asset channel**: Canadian pension/institutional portfolios lose value on USD-denominated tech holdings
- **Net effect**: reduces capital inflows to US, weakens USD somewhat, but also hurts Canadian wealth

### 3. Private Credit Unwinding
- $1.7T+ global private credit market facing liquidity stress
- Canadian banks and pension funds (CPPIB, CDPQ, OTPP) are major private credit allocators
- Margin calls and redemptions → forced selling of assets → need USD liquidity
- Creates sudden demand for USD funding → CAD weakens in the short run
- Similar to 2008 dynamic: Canadian entities need USD, creating FX funding stress
- **Net effect**: short-term CAD weakness from liquidity scramble (even if fundamentals are OK)

### 4. Bank of Canada vs Federal Reserve Divergence
- BoC cut to 2.75%; Fed still at 4.25-4.50%
- **175bp rate gap** — historically wide for CAD/USD
- UIP says: higher US rates attract capital to US → CAD weakens → but then CAD expected to appreciate
- This is the DOMINANT force on the exchange rate right now
- BoC cutting to support housing market and slowing economy
- Fed holding because US inflation stickier + strong labor market
- **Net effect**: main driver of CAD weakness to ~1.44

### 5. US Tariff Threats / Trade War Risk
- Ongoing US protectionist rhetoric; 25% tariff threats on Canadian goods
- Would devastate Canadian manufacturing and auto sector
- Shifts USD demand curve RIGHT (Canadians still need USD for imports, but US buys less from Canada)
- Simultaneously shifts USD supply curve LEFT (fewer US dollars coming to Canada for exports)
- **Net effect**: clearly bad for CAD; could push to 1.50+

## Section-by-Section Mapping

### Section 1: FX Market (Supply & Demand for USD)
- **Y-axis**: Exchange rate E = CAD per USD (higher = weaker CAD)
- **Demand for USD**: Canadians buying US goods, investing in US assets, paying USD debts
- **Supply of USD**: Americans buying Canadian goods, investing in Canada, buying oil
- **Realistic defaults**: E ≈ 1.44 CAD/USD
- **Try this**: Iran war → oil spike → supply shifts right (more USD from oil) → CAD strengthens
- **Try this**: Tariff threat → supply shifts left + demand stays → CAD weakens

### Section 2: Money → Interest → FX
- **Mˢ slider** = Bank of Canada money supply (BoC balance sheet / policy stance)
- **i* (foreign rate)** = Fed funds rate ≈ 4.25%
- **Eᵉ (expected future)** = where markets think CAD/USD is heading
- **BoC easing**: more money → lower Canadian rates → capital flows to US → CAD weakens
- **Current reality**: BoC at 2.75% vs Fed at 4.25% explains CAD at 1.44

### Section 3: Interest Rate Parity
- **Home rate** = BoC rate ≈ 2.75%
- **Foreign rate** = Fed rate ≈ 4.25%
- UIP says: CAD is weak NOW but should gradually strengthen (expected CAD appreciation compensates for lower rates)
- CIP: forward CAD/USD should be below spot (forward discount on USD / premium on CAD)
- Current 1Y forward: ~1.42 vs spot ~1.44 → consistent with CIP

### Section 4: PPP
- **Canada inflation** ≈ 2.5%, **US inflation** ≈ 2.8%
- PPP says: slightly lower Canadian inflation → CAD should slowly appreciate in real terms
- BUT current CAD is weaker than PPP suggests → standard finding (short-run deviations persist)
- **Starting E** = 1.44
- Big Mac Index: CAD roughly 10-15% undervalued vs USD

### Section 5: Balance of Payments
- Canada runs a **current account deficit** (~-2% GDP)
- Financed by capital inflows (US and global investors buying Canadian bonds, real estate, etc.)
- Energy exports partially offset goods import deficit
- **J-curve relevant**: if CAD depreciates 10%, Canadian trade balance worsens first (import prices spike), then improves as export volumes grow

### Section 6: Mundell-Fleming
- Canada = **FLOATING rate** + **free capital** → monetary policy is the effective tool
- **BoC rate cut** = monetary expansion → LM shifts right → lower rates → capital outflows → CAD depreciates → exports rise → IS shifts right → output increases → this is exactly what BoC is trying to do
- **Fiscal expansion** (deficit spending): under floating, appreciation offsets the stimulus → less effective → this is why Ottawa's fiscal plans have limited macro impact via the FX channel
- Compare to Eurozone members (fixed within euro, free capital): fiscal policy is more potent there

### Section 7: Overshooting
- **BoC surprise cut**: if BoC cuts more than expected, CAD overshoots — drops past its new long-run level, then gradually appreciates back
- Canadian prices are sticky (wages, rents, contracts denominated in CAD)
- This explains why CAD/USD moves 2-3% on rate announcement days but then drifts back
- **Private credit unwind**: sudden liquidity shock → massive overshoot as exchange rate absorbs the blow while prices haven't adjusted

### Section 8: Trilemma
- **Canada chose**: floating rate + free capital + monetary independence ← the "Free Capital + Policy Autonomy" option
- Sacrificed: fixed exchange rate (CAD floats freely)
- This is why BoC CAN cut when Fed holds — but the cost is a weaker CAD
- **If Canada tried to peg** to USD: would need to match Fed rates → couldn't cut to help housing market → economy would struggle

### Section 9: Crisis
- **Moderate vulnerability**: high bank USD-funding dependency, private credit exposure
- **Strong factors**: solid reserve credibility, AAA sovereign credit, transparent BoC
- **Scenario**: if private credit unwind triggers margin calls on Canadian institutions → sudden USD demand → CAD drops → USD-denominated debt burden rises → feedback loop
- **Mitigant**: BoC USD swap line with Fed, adequate reserves, credible policy framework
- Not crisis-like now, but the ingredients for stress are present
