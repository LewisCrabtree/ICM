Section 1

The Foreign Exchange Market

Before we can understand why the loonie moves, we need to see what an exchange rate actually is. CAD/USD is just a price — how many Canadian dollars it costs to buy one US dollar — set by supply and demand like any other market. Once you see the two curves, every section that follows is just a story about what shifts them.

  • Supply of USD (purple S curve): Americans sending USD to Canada — to buy Canadian oil/lumber, invest in TSX, purchase Canadian real estate

  • Every concept in this guide — interest rates, money supply, inflation, crises — ultimately works by shifting these two curves

The Bank of Canada sets money supply → that determines Canadian interest rates → which pin down CAD/USD through international capital flows.   • This two-panel diagram is THE core mechanism. When the BoC cuts rates, capital flows to the US because the Fed offers better returns → CAD weakens. The ~125bp BoC–Fed gap is why CAD went from 1.36 to 1.44 and has since recovered to ~1.39 as the Fed began cutting too
Section 3

Interest Rate Parity

Section 2 showed that higher US rates pull capital toward the US and weaken the loonie. But if that's true, why doesn't all capital flow to the US? There must be an equilibrium. Interest rate parity formalizes the answer: the market prices in expected CAD appreciation to compensate for the rate gap — and that arbitrage condition pins down today's exchange rate.

  • Fed Rate (i*): US rate at 3.50–3.75%. Embedded in the green downward-sloping curve — the expected return from holding USD deposits
  • Expected Future E (Eᵉ): where markets think CAD/USD will be. Shifts the green curve — higher Eᵉ (weaker future loonie) shifts it up

How to read the chart

  • Where they cross = equilibrium exchange rate. The market adjusts E until investors are indifferent between CAD and USD deposits

Key takeaway

  • The ~125bp BoC–Fed gap is why CAD is somewhat weak. But parity says this weakness is temporary — CAD must be expected to appreciate to make CAD deposits competitive. The loonie is cheap now precisely because markets expect it to recover

The short-run forces eventually fade, and a deeper anchor takes over: inflation. PPP says the long-run destination lives in the inflation gap between Canada and the US — giving us the endpoint that the short-run dynamics are converging toward.

Section 5

Balance of Payments

So far we've tracked exchange rates through money, interest rates, and prices. But there's a parallel accounting reality we've been ignoring: trade. Every dollar Canada spends on US imports must come back as US investment in Canada — the BOP always balances. This section connects the capital-flow story from Sections 2–3 with the real-world flow of goods, and shows what happens to trade when the currency moves (the J-curve).

BOP Identity: CA + FA = 0

J-Curve: Canada Trade Balance After CAD Weakens

Canada Current Account—
Canada Financial Account—

What the controls mean

  • Trade Balance (CA): Canada's current account — exports minus imports. Negative = deficit (Canada imports more than it exports). Currently ~−C$15B per quarter
  • Rate Premium (BoC − Fed): the interest rate gap. At −1.4% (BoC 2.25% vs Fed ~3.6%), US assets are more attractive. This drives the Financial Account — capital flows IN or OUT of Canada
  • CAD Depreciation Shock: a sudden weakening of the loonie (e.g., from tariff news). This triggers the J-curve in the right panel

How to read the two panels

  • Left panel (bars): Current Account (cyan) + Financial Account (purple) must always = 0. If Canada runs a trade deficit, it MUST attract foreign capital (positive FA) to pay for it
  • Right panel (J-curve): after a CAD depreciation, imports get more expensive immediately (trade balance worsens), but over time Canadian exports become more competitive and volumes adjust (trade balance improves). The dip-then-recovery shape = the J

🔬 Try This — Current events

  • BoC–Fed divergence: set Rate Premium to −1.5. The bars show a capital outflow from Canada (negative FA?) — actually, because Canada needs to attract capital to fund its trade deficit, the rate premium makes this harder. This is why the loonie is under pressure
  • Iran war oil price spike: drag Trade Balance from −1.5 to +2.0. Canada is a net energy exporter — $100+ oil swings the CA into surplus. Now Canada is lending to the world (negative FA). Oil booms are good for the loonie
  • US tariffs on Canadian goods: drag Trade Balance from −1.5 → −4.0. Exports collapse → bigger deficit → Canada needs even more foreign capital → FA must grow → but with lower rates it's hard to attract capital → currency must weaken to make Canadian assets cheaper
  • J-curve after tariff shock: set depreciation to 15%. Initially the trade balance tanks (import bill jumps), but over 6–12 months Canadian products become cheaper for Americans and volumes adjust. The loonie weakness is the economy's immune response

Key takeaway

  • Every dollar Canada spends on US imports must come back as US investment in Canada. The BOP always balances. The question is: does Canada attract capital through high rates (no — BoC is low) or through a cheap currency (yes — that's what 1.39 is doing)
Section 6

The Mundell-Fleming Model (IS-LM-BP)

We now have all the building blocks: money markets (Section 2), capital flows (Section 3), trade (Section 5). Mundell-Fleming ties them together into one diagram to answer the big policy question: when the BoC cuts rates or Ottawa spends billions, what actually happens to output? The answer depends entirely on the exchange rate regime — and Canada's choice to float means monetary policy is king while fiscal stimulus leaks through the exchange rate.

Exchange Rate:
Policy Shock:
Policy Verdict—
Output (Y)—
Interest Rate (i)—
  • IS (cyan) = goods market equilibrium; LM (purple) = money market; BP (green) = external balance at world interest rate
  • Under FLOATING + capital mobility: monetary policy is powerful (depreciation boosts exports); fiscal policy is ineffective (appreciation cancels the stimulus)
  • Under FIXED + capital mobility: fiscal policy is powerful (CB must accommodate); monetary policy is impossible (CB must reverse to defend the peg)
  • Why monetary policy fails under fixed rates: expansion → lower rates → capital outflows → currency pressure → CB sells reserves → money supply goes back
  • Why fiscal policy fails under floating: stimulus → higher rates → capital inflows → appreciation → exports fall → output back to start
  • The key result: "floating rates empower central banks; fixed rates empower finance ministers"
Section 7

Exchange Rate Overshooting

Mundell-Fleming told us which direction the exchange rate moves after a policy shock. But it didn't explain how much — and in practice, FX markets wildly overreact. When the BoC surprises with a cut, CAD doesn't gently drift to its new level — it plunges past it, then slowly recovers. Dornbusch (1976) explains why: goods prices are sticky, so the exchange rate must absorb the entire shock alone, temporarily overshooting to keep markets in equilibrium.

Long-Run CAD/USD Change—

What the controls mean

  • BoC Money Supply Increase (%): how big is the monetary expansion. 15% is a large shock (like cumulative QE). The bigger the increase, the bigger the overshoot
  • Price Stickiness (θ): how fast Canadian prices (wages, rent, groceries) adjust. Low θ = very sticky prices (slow adjustment) = MORE overshooting. High θ = prices adjust quickly = less overshoot

How to read the chart

  • The red spike is the initial CAD/USD jump — the currency overshoots past the dashed long-run line
  • The dashed horizontal shows where CAD/USD eventually settles (the long-run PPP-consistent level)
  • The recovery path slopes downward as prices gradually adjust and the exchange rate comes back from its overshoot

🔬 Try This — The October 2024 BoC jumbo cut

  • The real event: In Oct 2024, BoC cut by 50bp in one shot. Set Money Supply to ~10% and θ to 0.5. Watch the overshoot — CAD weakened from ~1.38 to 1.44 within weeks, overshooting the long-run level. This model explains why
  • Why overshoot happens: Prices of Tim Hortons coffee, Toronto rent, and Ontario auto parts don't change overnight. So the exchange rate must do ALL the adjusting alone — and it overshoots to generate expected appreciation that makes investors willing to hold lower-yielding CAD assets
  • Stickier prices = bigger swings: drag θ from 0.5 → 0.15. The overshoot gets enormous. In any economy where prices adjust slowly relative to asset markets, the exchange rate must do more of the heavy lifting — amplifying volatility
  • Private credit / AI bubble pop: if a major financial shock hit and BoC had to do emergency easing (Money Supply +30%), the overshoot would be massive — CAD could spike to 1.55+ before recovering. This is the scenario Canada's financial system fears

Key takeaway

  • Exchange rate volatility is not irrational — it's the rational consequence of sticky prices. The CAD/USD must overshoot so investors expect appreciation, which compensates them for holding lower-yielding Canadian deposits during the transition. This explains why FX markets move so much more than fundamentals seem to justify
Section 8

The Impossible Trinity

Sections 6–7 assumed Canada floats its dollar. But why does Canada float? Is that the only option? The impossible trinity reveals the fundamental constraint: countries can have at most two of fixed rates, free capital, and independent monetary policy. Canada chose free capital + BoC autonomy, and the floating CAD is the price. This section shows what other countries chose — and why every regime is a tradeoff.

What the three corners mean

  • Fixed exchange rate: peg your currency to another (e.g., HKD pegged to USD). Gives stability but removes exchange rate as a shock absorber
  • Free capital mobility: let money flow across borders freely. Enables investment but makes you vulnerable to sudden stops
  • Independent monetary policy: set your own interest rates based on domestic conditions. Gives the CB power to fight recessions/inflation
  • The triangle says: pick any two, the third is impossible

The three scenarios

  • Fixed + Free Capital (sacrifice policy): Hong Kong, Eurozone. The CB must match the anchor country's rates exactly. If the Fed hikes, HK must hike too, even if their economy is weak
  • Free Capital + Policy Autonomy (sacrifice fixed rate): Canada, US, UK, Japan. The BoC sets rates independently, but CAD/USD must float. This is Canada's choice — it's why we have a 1.39 rate instead of a peg
  • Fixed + Policy Autonomy (sacrifice capital mobility): China historically, Malaysia 1998. You control capital flows with regulations. Beijing can set rates AND peg the yuan, but companies can't freely move money in/out

🔬 Try This — Why Canada floats

  • Click "Free Capital + Policy Autonomy" — this is Canada. The BoC can cut to 2.25% while the Fed sits at 3.50–3.75% because Canada accepts that CAD floats to 1.39. If Canada pegged at 1.00, BoC would be forced to match the Fed rate
  • Click "Fixed + Free Capital" — this is Hong Kong or EU members. When the Fed hiked aggressively in 2022-23, HK rates automatically followed even though their property market was crashing. No independent policy
  • Click "Fixed + Policy Autonomy" — this is China. The PBoC can ease while maintaining a stable yuan — but only because capital controls prevent money from fleeing. This is why Chinese investors struggle to buy Toronto real estate directly (they use workarounds)
  • Think about the private credit unwind: if US shadow banking stress triggers capital flight from Canada, having a floating rate is actually protection — CAD weakens, which automatically makes Canadian assets cheaper for foreign bargain hunters, attracting capital back. A peg would break instead

Key takeaway

  • Every time CAD drops and someone asks "why doesn't Canada just peg to the USD?" — the answer is the trinity. Pegging would mean surrendering the BoC's ability to set rates for Canadian conditions. Canada chose policy freedom, and the volatile CAD is the cost
Section 9

Currency Crises & Feedback Loops

Everything up to now described orderly adjustment — markets finding equilibrium through arbitrage and policy. But what happens when the system breaks? When private credit unwinds, funding dries up, and balance sheets are denominated in USD, the parity conditions from Sections 2–4 stop holding. Vicious circles take over: currency falls → debt burden rises → more selling → currency falls further. This section simulates the mechanics of that breakdown.

What the controls mean

  • Funding Shock: how severe is the sudden stop of capital. 0 = calm markets, 10 = full-blown GFC-style panic. Think: private credit fund freezing redemptions, collateralized loan obligations (CLOs) blowing up, or Iran war risk premium spike
  • BoC Reserve Credibility: how much the market trusts Canada's central bank to manage the crisis. Canada scores high (8/10) due to transparent policy, AAA fiscal credit, and US swap lines. An emerging market might score 3-4
  • USD-Denominated Debt: the percentage of Canadian corporate/sovereign debt that must be repaid in USD. When CAD weakens, this debt gets MORE expensive in CAD terms — the vicious circle. Canada's exposure is ~30% (moderate; EM countries can be 60%+)

How the feedback loop works

  • Funding shock (e.g., US private credit freeze) → investors pull capital from Canada → CAD weakens
  • CAD weakening → USD-denominated debt becomes more expensive in CAD terms → Canadian firms under stress → credit downgrades
  • Credit downgrades → more capital flight → CAD weakens further → vicious circle
  • The circle breaks when: reserves are credible enough, the shock dissipates, or external help arrives (Fed swap lines, IMF)

🔬 Try This — Stress test scenarios

  • Canada baseline (resilient): Shock 4, Reserves 8, USD Debt 30%. The feedback loop is contained — CAD weakens moderately but doesn't spiral. This is Canada's current position: strong institutions buffer the shock
  • Private credit unwind hits: drag Shock to 7 (US private credit/CLO market freezes, CMBS stress). Keep Reserves at 8, USD Debt at 30%. Now watch — the stress rises but Canada's credibility keeps it manageable. The BoC would activate Fed swap lines
  • What if Canada were an emerging market? Set Reserves to 3, USD Debt to 65%, Shock to 6. Now the feedback loop explodes — cascading defaults, currency in freefall, sovereign bond yields spike. This is what happened to Turkey 2018, Argentina 2001, and Asia 1997
  • Iran war extreme: Shock 8, Reserves 8, USD Debt 30%. Even with strong institutions, a severe enough shock (oil at $120 then collapsing, geopolitical chaos) can cause material stress. CAD could hit 1.50+, corporate refinancing gets painful
  • AI bubble pop + private credit + Iran (tail risk combo): Shock 9, Reserves 7 (slightly eroded by political uncertainty), USD Debt 35%. This is the "everything breaks" scenario. Even Canada's strong system would need emergency measures

Key takeaway

  • Canada is well-positioned: strong institutions, moderate USD exposure, Fed swap line access. But no country is immune to a large enough shock. The private credit market (~$1.7T US) is the current systemic risk that could trigger the next funding shock. Watch CLO spreads, HY bond yields, and bank earnings for early warning signs
Section 10

Putting It All Together — Canada vs USA, March 2026

You've now seen every piece: how central banks move rates (2), how rates move exchange rates (3), where they're headed long-run (4), how trade adjusts (5), which policies work (6), why markets overreact (7), what constrains regime choice (8), and when it all breaks down (9). This final section uses those nine frameworks as a lens to explain why every key Canadian number is where it is right now — and what the theory says happens next.

The question this section answers: You see the headlines — BoC at 2.25%, Fed at 3.50–3.75%, CAD/USD at 1.39, 5Y fixed mortgages at ~4.0%, variable mortgages at ~3.35–4.45%. Why are they at those exact numbers, and what do the models from Sections 1–9 predict happens next?

Part A — Why every number is where it is

🔗 The BoC–Fed gap: ~125bp (Sections 2 & 8)

  • Section 2 says: when the BoC increases money supply (easing cycle from 5% → 2.25%), the left panel shows Canadian interest rates falling. The right panel shows the CAD return line shifting left → E rises → CAD weakens
  • Section 8 says: Canada can only do this because it chose the "free capital + policy autonomy" corner of the trinity. The floating CAD is the pressure valve that makes independent rate cuts possible. If Canada pegged to the USD, the BoC would be forced to hold at 3.50–3.75% alongside the Fed — imagine the pain for Canadian mortgage holders
  • Why ~125bp? The BoC cut aggressively because Canada's economy was weaker — GDP growth ~1%, housing market fragile, consumer debt at record ~185% of disposable income. The Fed eventually followed with three cuts in 2025, but US inflation remained stickier at 2.4% with a resilient labor market. Two very different economies, converging but still divergent policies — and the remaining gap drives everything below

🔗 CAD/USD at 1.39 (Sections 1, 3 & 7)

  • Section 1 says: the ~125bp gap increases demand for USD (Canadians chasing higher yields) and decreases supply of USD (Americans have less reason to invest in lower-yield Canada). Both shifts push E up → 1.39
  • Section 3 (UIP) says: 1.39 is the exact rate where investors are indifferent between CAD and USD deposits. At 1.39, the expected CAD appreciation (~1.3% per year back toward ~1.37) compensates for the ~125bp you're giving up vs the Fed. If CAD were at 1.34, investors would expect CAD to weaken toward 1.37 — so on top of losing on the rate gap, you'd lose on FX too, and everyone would pile into USD. If at 1.45, the expected appreciation would be too generous and capital would flood back to CAD. 1.39 is the equilibrium
  • Section 7 says: CAD likely overshot on the way to its trough. When the BoC surprised with the 50bp cut in Oct 2024, goods prices didn't budge overnight — so the exchange rate absorbed the full blow alone, spiking to ~1.46 before gradually recovering to 1.39 as the Fed caught up. The overshoot is why FX markets are so volatile around rate decisions

🔗 Canada's trade balance (Section 5)

  • The BOP identity: Canada runs a current account deficit of ~2% GDP — importing more goods/services than it exports. This must be financed by capital inflows (Americans and others buying Canadian bonds, real estate, or corporate debt)
  • But with the BoC at 2.25% vs Fed at 3.50–3.75%, attracting that capital is harder. Canada can't offer competitive yields → so the currency must weaken to make Canadian assets cheaper for foreign buyers. This is the BOP mechanism reinforcing the rate differential story
  • The one bright spot: oil above $100 from Iran war risk helps Canada's trade balance (energy is ~20% of exports). Section 1 manifests: oil revenue shifts the USD supply curve right, partially supporting CAD. Without high oil, CAD might be at 1.45+

🔗 Why Ottawa can't "fix" this (Section 6)

  • Mundell-Fleming's brutal lesson: under floating rates with free capital, fiscal policy is ineffective. If Ottawa announces a $50B stimulus, rates would edge up → capital floods in → CAD strengthens → exports collapse → stimulus nullified. The exchange rate undoes the fiscal push
  • This is why the BoC — not Parliament — is the only institution that can meaningfully steer the Canadian economy right now. The BoC's rate cuts are working through the weak CAD: cheaper loonie → more competitive exports → IS shifts right. The policy framework from Section 6 is playing out in real time

Part B — What happens next? The models from Sections 2–9 don't just explain today. They make specific, testable predictions about the next 1–5 years for Canadian interest rates, the exchange rate, and — crucially — what Canadians care about most: mortgages.

📉 Variable-Rate Mortgages

Priced off the BoC overnight rate (currently 2.25%). Best variable rates sit at ~3.35% (prime − 1.10%). Section 2 predicts: if the BoC holds at 2.25% or eases further if the economy weakens, variable rates remain attractive. Variable holders have already benefited enormously from the BoC's 275bp of cuts since mid-2024.

📊 Fixed-Rate Mortgages (5Y)

Priced off the 5Y Government of Canada bond yield (~3.2%), NOT the BoC rate. Section 3 (parity) + Section 4 (PPP) predict: the 5Y GoC yield blends BoC rate expectations AND long-run inflation expectations. Even if the BoC cuts more, 5Y fixed rates may not fall much — because bond markets already price in BoC cuts, and long-run inflation is anchored near 2%. Current best ~4.0% fixed rates may settle around 3.8–4.2%.

💱 CAD/USD Exchange Rate

Section 3 (UIP): the BoC–Fed gap has already narrowed from ~175bp to ~125bp as the Fed caught up. If the gap continues closing (Fed cuts more or BoC holds), capital flows rebalance and CAD strengthens toward ~1.34–1.37. Section 4 (PPP): lower Canadian inflation (1.8% vs 2.4%) suggests the long-run anchor for CAD/USD is ~1.32–1.34. Both forces point to continued CAD strengthening — the recovery from 1.44 to 1.39 may be just the beginning.

⚠️ Risk Scenarios

Section 7 (Overshooting): any BoC emergency cut in that scenario would cause CAD to overshoot well beyond its new equilibrium. Fixed mortgage holders are protected; variable holders are helped but face imported inflation.

  • Next 3–6 months (Sections 2, 3): CAD/USD likely stays in the 1.36–1.42 range. The BoC–Fed gap is narrowing, supporting CAD. If the Fed signals further cuts, CAD strengthens toward 1.35 rapidly (Section 3 — UIP recalibrates instantly on expectations).   • 12–24 months (Sections 3, 6): Markets price the BoC holding near 2.25% and the Fed potentially cutting to ~3.0% by late 2027. Mundell-Fleming confirms: BoC's rate cuts have already done their stimulative work through the weak CAD. As the economy stabilizes, the BoC can hold and let the rate gap close naturally
  • 3–5 years (Section 4 — PPP): The long-run anchor. Canada's lower inflation (1.8% vs 2.4%) means the PPP trend favors a gradually stronger CAD. The 10Y PPP-implied rate is ~1.32. CAD will likely drift towards this range — but PPP takes years, not months, and can be overwhelmed by capital flow shocks in the meantime

The interest rate forecast, by instrument

  • BoC overnight rate: Currently 2.25%. The BoC has held since its last cut in October 2025. If Canada slides into recession, could go to 1.75% or below. Section 2: each cut further weakens CAD, but also stimulates through exports (Section 6). The BoC is walking a tightrope — cut too much and CAD at 1.45+ creates import inflation; cut too little and housing/consumer debt implodes
  • 5Y GoC bond yield: Currently ~3.2%. Reflects BoC rate expectations + term premium + inflation expectations. Even with BoC cuts, unlikely to drop below 2.5–2.8% because the long-run inflation anchor is firm and Iran war fears are adding a risk premium.   • Variable mortgage rates: Best currently ~3.35% (prime 4.45% minus 1.10%). Move in lockstep with BoC. If BoC reaches 1.75%, prime drops to ~3.95%, so variable rates settle near ~2.85–3.95%. Variable has been the winning bet since the BoC started cutting
  • Fixed 5Y mortgage rates: Best currently ~4.0%. Priced off the 5Y GoC bond + lender spread (~80–150bp). Even if BoC cuts aggressively, 5Y bond yields may not fall much due to Iran war inflation fears. Forecast: 3.7–4.2% range. Fixed wins if you think bond yields will rise from here or if you want payment certainty

The mortgage decision through the lens of Sections 2–7

  • Choose variable if (Section 2 worldview): you believe the BoC will cut further because Canada's economy is weak. Every 25bp BoC cut flows directly to your payment. Risk: if inflation re-accelerates (Iran oil shock → imported inflation via weak CAD), the BoC reverses course and your rate spikes. Section 7 is your risk: the exchange rate could overshoot, causing import prices to surge, forcing the BoC to pause or hike
  • Choose fixed if (Section 4 worldview): you believe we're near the trough and rates will normalize upward. The 5Y GoC yield already prices in the BoC staying low — you're locking in the market's best guess of the average rate over 5 years. Section 9 is your insurance: if a private credit crisis or tariff war triggers economic chaos, your payment doesn't change while variable holders face uncertainty
  • The spread matters: currently variable can be as low as ~3.35% vs fixed ~4.0% — a ~65bp gap in favor of variable. Historically, variable beats fixed ~80% of the time because the yield curve is usually upward-sloping and BoC doesn't hike as much as bonds fear. With the BoC rate well below the 5Y yield, markets expect rates to eventually rise or stay near current levels. The gap reflects the market's bet that the BoC will hold, then eventually normalize upward

Putting the current shocks together — the complete picture

  • BoC vs Fed divergence (Sections 2, 3): a narrowing force. The gap has compressed from ~175bp to ~125bp as the Fed cut three times in 2025. Every Fed cut without a matching BoC cut narrows the gap and supports CAD. Prediction: this gap narrows further in 2027 as the Fed continues easing toward 3.0%
  • Iran war / oil shock (Sections 1, 5): oil above $100 supports Canada's trade balance and partially offsets the remaining BoC–Fed rate gap. Net effect on CAD: mixed — oil revenue helps, but war-driven risk-off strengthens the safe-haven USD. Risk: if oil spikes to $120 then crashes, Canada's trade balance deteriorates → CAD weakens → and the J-curve from Section 5 kicks in (import bill rises before export volumes adjust)
  • AI bubble concerns (Sections 1, 9): a 30%+ US tech correction would reduce capital inflows to the US (Section 1 — demand for USD drops). But also creates risk-off panic that hurts all non-US assets. For mortgages: a correction would likely cause BoC to cut faster → variable rates fall → but also triggers financial stress that tightens lending standards
  • Private credit unwind (Section 9): the tail risk. $1.7T US market with opacity and leverage. Canadian pensions (CPPIB, CDPQ, OTPP) are deep in this space. If CLOs freeze, the funding shock from Section 9 materializes → CAD overshoots to 1.50+ (Section 7) → BoC forced into emergency cuts → variable mortgage holders see rates plunge but face a weak-CAD import inflation hit on everything else
  • US tariffs (Sections 1, 5, 6): 25% tariffs would crush Canadian exports (Section 5 — trade balance to −4 or worse), force CAD to perhaps 1.50+ (Section 1), but be partially absorbed by Mundell-Fleming's automatic stabilizer (Section 6 — weaker CAD makes remaining exports competitive). For mortgages: tariff-driven recession → BoC cuts deeper → variable rates fall sharply → but housing values also fall. The worst scenario for homeowners is tariffs + BoC hikes to defend CAD — but Section 8 tells us Canada won't do that (floating rate means BoC targets the domestic economy, not the exchange rate)
