The Foreign Exchange Market

The CAD/USD exchange rate is just a price — how many Canadian dollars it costs to buy one US dollar — set by supply and demand.

What the controls mean

  • Demand for USD (cyan D curve): Canadians wanting USD — to import American goods, buy US stocks, pay USD debts, travel to the US
  • Supply of USD (purple S curve): Americans sending USD to Canada — to buy Canadian oil/lumber, invest in TSX, purchase Canadian real estate

Key takeaway

  • Every concept in this guide — interest rates, money supply, inflation, crises — ultimately works by shifting these two curves

Money Supply → Interest Rates → Exchange Rates

The core transmission chain: the Bank of Canada sets money supply → this determines Canadian interest rates → which pin down the CAD/USD rate through capital flows.

What the controls mean

  • BoC Money Supply (Mˢ): how much money the Bank of Canada puts into the system. Larger = more easing. The vertical line in the left panel
  • Fed Rate (i*): the US Federal Reserve interest rate. This is the benchmark that Canadian returns compete against. It positions the green foreign-return curve in the right panel
  • Expected Future CAD/USD (Eᵉ): where the market thinks the exchange rate will be in the future. Shifts the green curve up/down

How to read the two panels

  • Left panel: the money market. Where BoC's money supply (vertical cyan line) crosses money demand (pink curve) gives the Canadian interest rate
  • Right panel: the returns comparison. The vertical cyan line = Canadian return (from the left panel). The green downward curve = expected return from USD deposits. Where they cross = equilibrium CAD/USD
  • The green curve slopes down because: the weaker CAD is today, the less room for further weakening, so the less attractive USD deposits become

🔬 Try This — BoC vs Fed divergence (the dominant story of 2025-26)

  • BoC easing: drag Mˢ from 7.0 → 9.0. Watch the Canadian rate drop in the left panel. Then watch the cyan return line shift LEFT in the right panel → equilibrium E rises → CAD weakens. This is exactly what happened as BoC cut from 5% to 2.75% while the Fed held at 4.25%
  • Fed surprise cut: drag Fed Rate from 4.3% → 3.0%. The green foreign-return curve shifts LEFT. Even without BoC doing anything, E drops → CAD strengthens. Markets would celebrate this for Canada
  • Private credit panic / risk-off: drag Eᵉ from 1.40 → 1.55 (markets expect CAD to be weaker in the future). The green curve shifts UP → E rises immediately → CAD weakens now because investors demand compensation for expected future losses

Key takeaway

  • This two-panel diagram is THE core mechanism. When the BoC cuts rates, capital flows to the US because the Fed offers better returns → CAD weakens. The 175bp BoC–Fed gap is why CAD went from 1.36 to 1.44

Interest Rate Parity

Why doesn't all capital flow to the US just because the Fed rate is higher? Because the market prices in expected CAD appreciation to compensate.

What the controls mean

  • BoC Rate (i): Canadian overnight rate at 2.75%. This is the vertical cyan line — the return you earn holding CAD deposits
  • Fed Rate (i*): US rate at 4.25%. Embedded in the green downward-sloping curve — the expected return from holding USD deposits
  • Expected Future E (Eᵉ): where markets think CAD/USD will be. Shifts the green curve — higher Eᵉ (weaker future loonie) shifts it up
  • Toggle UIP/CIP: UIP = risk-bearing bet on expected spot; CIP = risk-free forward contract (locked in today)

How to read the chart

  • The cyan vertical line = Canadian interest rate = return on CAD deposits
  • The green downward curve = expected return on USD deposits (in CAD terms). It slopes down because: the weaker CAD is today (high E), the more CAD-appreciation you'd expect, so USD deposits look less attractive
  • Where they cross = equilibrium exchange rate. The market adjusts E until investors are indifferent between CAD and USD deposits

  • What if BoC cuts more? Drag BoC rate from 2.8% → 1.5%. The cyan line shifts left → E* jumps higher → CAD weakens further. This is exactly what markets fear if the Canadian recession deepens
  • What if Fed pivots? Drag Fed rate from 4.3% → 3.0%. The green curve shifts left → E* drops → CAD surges. A Fed cut would be the single biggest positive catalyst for the loonie

Key takeaway

  • The 175bp BoC–Fed gap is why CAD is weak. But parity says this weakness is temporary — CAD must be expected to appreciate to make CAD deposits competitive. The loonie is cheap now precisely because markets expect it to recover

Section 5

Balance of Payments

Canada's trade with the world must add up. If Canada sends more C$ out for imports than it earns from exports, the difference must flow back as capital investment. Always.

BOP Identity: CA + FA = 0

J-Curve: Canada Trade Balance After CAD Weakens

Canada Current Account—
Canada Financial Account—

What the controls mean

  • Trade Balance (CA): Canada's current account — exports minus imports. Negative = deficit (Canada imports more than it exports). Currently ~−C$15B per quarter
  • Rate Premium (BoC − Fed): the interest rate gap. At −1.5% (BoC 2.75% vs Fed 4.25%), US assets are more attractive. This drives the Financial Account — capital flows IN or OUT of Canada
  • CAD Depreciation Shock: a sudden weakening of the loonie (e.g., from tariff news). This triggers the J-curve in the right panel

How to read the two panels

  • Left panel (bars): Current Account (cyan) + Financial Account (purple) must always = 0. If Canada runs a trade deficit, it MUST attract foreign capital (positive FA) to pay for it
  • Right panel (J-curve): after a CAD depreciation, imports get more expensive immediately (trade balance worsens), but over time Canadian exports become more competitive and volumes adjust (trade balance improves). The dip-then-recovery shape = the J

🔬 Try This — Current events

  • BoC–Fed divergence: set Rate Premium to −1.5. The bars show a capital outflow from Canada (negative FA?) — actually, because Canada needs to attract capital to fund its trade deficit, the rate premium makes this harder. This is why the loonie is under pressure
  • Iran war oil price spike: drag Trade Balance from −1.5 to +2.0. Canada is a net energy exporter — $90+ oil swings the CA into surplus. Now Canada is lending to the world (negative FA). Oil booms are good for the loonie
  • US tariffs on Canadian goods: drag Trade Balance from −1.5 → −4.0. Exports collapse → bigger deficit → Canada needs even more foreign capital → FA must grow → but with lower rates it's hard to attract capital → currency must weaken to make Canadian assets cheaper
  • J-curve after tariff shock: set depreciation to 15%. Initially the trade balance tanks (import bill jumps), but over 6–12 months Canadian products become cheaper for Americans and volumes adjust. The loonie weakness is the economy's immune response

Key takeaway

  • Every dollar Canada spends on US imports must come back as US investment in Canada. The BOP always balances. The question is: does Canada attract capital through high rates (no — BoC is low) or through a cheap currency (yes — that's what 1.44 is doing)

The Mundell-Fleming Model (IS-LM-BP)

The workhorse model of open-economy macro: three curves show how fiscal and monetary policy work differently under fixed vs. floating exchange rates.

Policy Verdict—

  • IS (cyan) = goods market equilibrium; LM (purple) = money market; BP (green) = external balance at world interest rate
  • Under FLOATING + capital mobility: monetary policy is powerful (depreciation boosts exports); fiscal policy is ineffective (appreciation cancels the stimulus)
  • Under FIXED + capital mobility: fiscal policy is powerful (CB must accommodate); monetary policy is impossible (CB must reverse to defend the peg)
  • Why monetary policy fails under fixed rates: expansion → lower rates → capital outflows → currency pressure → CB sells reserves → money supply goes back
  • Why fiscal policy fails under floating: stimulus → higher rates → capital inflows → appreciation → exports fall → output back to start
  • The key result: "floating rates empower central banks; fixed rates empower finance ministers"
Section 7

Exchange Rate Overshooting

When the BoC surprises with a cut, CAD doesn't gently drift — it plunges PAST its new equilibrium, then slowly recovers. Dornbusch (1976) explains why: prices are sticky, so the exchange rate absorbs the entire shock alone.

Initial CAD/USD Jump (overshoot)—
Long-Run CAD/USD Change—

What the controls mean

  • BoC Money Supply Increase (%): how big is the monetary expansion. 15% is a large shock (like cumulative QE). The bigger the increase, the bigger the overshoot
  • Price Stickiness (θ): how fast Canadian prices (wages, rent, groceries) adjust. Low θ = very sticky prices (slow adjustment) = MORE overshooting. High θ = prices adjust quickly = less overshoot

How to read the chart

  • The red spike is the initial CAD/USD jump — the currency overshoots past the dashed long-run line
  • The dashed horizontal shows where CAD/USD eventually settles (the long-run PPP-consistent level)
  • The recovery path slopes downward as prices gradually adjust and the exchange rate comes back from its overshoot

🔬 Try This — The October 2024 BoC jumbo cut

  • The real event: In Oct 2024, BoC cut by 50bp in one shot. Set Money Supply to ~10% and θ to 0.5. Watch the overshoot — CAD weakened from ~1.38 to 1.44 within weeks, overshooting the long-run level. This model explains why
  • Why overshoot happens: Prices of Tim Hortons coffee, Toronto rent, and Ontario auto parts don't change overnight. So the exchange rate must do ALL the adjusting alone — and it overshoots to generate expected appreciation that makes investors willing to hold lower-yielding CAD assets
  • Stickier prices = bigger swings: drag θ from 0.5 → 0.15. The overshoot gets enormous. This is why emerging markets with very sticky prices see extreme currency volatility
  • Private credit / AI bubble pop: if a major financial shock hit and BoC had to do emergency easing (Money Supply +30%), the overshoot would be massive — CAD could spike to 1.55+ before recovering. This is the scenario Canada's financial system fears

Key takeaway

  • Exchange rate volatility is not irrational — it's the rational consequence of sticky prices. The CAD/USD must overshoot so investors expect appreciation, which compensates them for holding lower-yielding Canadian deposits during the transition. This explains why FX markets move so much more than fundamentals seem to justify
Section 8

The Impossible Trinity

Canada chose two: free capital flows + independent BoC policy. The price? A floating CAD/USD. No country gets all three. Click to see what other countries chose.

What the three corners mean

  • Fixed exchange rate: peg your currency to another (e.g., HKD pegged to USD). Gives stability but removes exchange rate as a shock absorber
  • Free capital mobility: let money flow across borders freely. Enables investment but makes you vulnerable to sudden stops
  • Independent monetary policy: set your own interest rates based on domestic conditions. Gives the CB power to fight recessions/inflation
  • The triangle says: pick any two, the third is impossible

The three scenarios

  • Fixed + Free Capital (sacrifice policy): Hong Kong, Eurozone. The CB must match the anchor country's rates exactly. If the Fed hikes, HK must hike too, even if their economy is weak
  • Free Capital + Policy Autonomy (sacrifice fixed rate): Canada, US, UK, Japan. The BoC sets rates independently, but CAD/USD must float. This is Canada's choice — it's why we have a 1.44 rate instead of a peg
  • Fixed + Policy Autonomy (sacrifice capital mobility): China historically, Malaysia 1998. You control capital flows with regulations. Beijing can set rates AND peg the yuan, but companies can't freely move money in/out

🔬 Try This — Why Canada floats

  • Click "Free Capital + Policy Autonomy" — this is Canada. The BoC can cut to 2.75% while the Fed sits at 4.25% because Canada accepts that CAD floats to 1.44. If Canada pegged at 1.00, BoC would be forced to match the Fed rate
  • Click "Fixed + Free Capital" — this is Hong Kong or EU members. When the Fed hiked aggressively in 2022-23, HK rates automatically followed even though their property market was crashing. No independent policy
  • Click "Fixed + Policy Autonomy" — this is China. The PBoC can ease while maintaining a stable yuan — but only because capital controls prevent money from fleeing. This is why Chinese investors struggle to buy Toronto real estate directly (they use workarounds)
  • Think about the private credit unwind: if US shadow banking stress triggers capital flight from Canada, having a floating rate is actually protection — CAD weakens, which automatically makes Canadian assets cheaper for foreign bargain hunters, attracting capital back. A peg would break instead

Key takeaway

  • Every time CAD drops and someone asks "why doesn't Canada just peg to the USD?" — the answer is the trinity. Pegging would mean surrendering the BoC's ability to set rates for Canadian conditions. Canada chose policy freedom, and the volatile CAD is the cost
Section 9

Currency Crises & Feedback Loops

When private credit unwinds, funding dries up, and balance sheets are in USD — orderly parity breaks down. Vicious circles take over. This section simulates the mechanics.

What the controls mean

  • Funding Shock: how severe is the sudden stop of capital. 0 = calm markets, 10 = full-blown GFC-style panic. Think: private credit fund freezing redemptions, collateralized loan obligations (CLOs) blowing up, or Iran war risk premium spike
  • BoC Reserve Credibility: how much the market trusts Canada's central bank to manage the crisis. Canada scores high (8/10) due to transparent policy, AAA fiscal credit, and US swap lines. An emerging market might score 3-4
  • USD-Denominated Debt: the percentage of Canadian corporate/sovereign debt that must be repaid in USD. When CAD weakens, this debt gets MORE expensive in CAD terms — the vicious circle. Canada's exposure is ~30% (moderate; EM countries can be 60%+)

How the feedback loop works

  • Funding shock (e.g., US private credit freeze) → investors pull capital from Canada → CAD weakens
  • CAD weakening → USD-denominated debt becomes more expensive in CAD terms → Canadian firms under stress → credit downgrades
  • Credit downgrades → more capital flight → CAD weakens further → vicious circle
  • The circle breaks when: reserves are credible enough, the shock dissipates, or external help arrives (Fed swap lines, IMF)

🔬 Try This — Stress test scenarios

  • Canada baseline (resilient): Shock 4, Reserves 8, USD Debt 30%. The feedback loop is contained — CAD weakens moderately but doesn't spiral. This is Canada's current position: strong institutions buffer the shock
  • Private credit unwind hits: drag Shock to 7 (US private credit/CLO market freezes, CMBS stress). Keep Reserves at 8, USD Debt at 30%. Now watch — the stress rises but Canada's credibility keeps it manageable. The BoC would activate Fed swap lines
  • What if Canada were an emerging market? Set Reserves to 3, USD Debt to 65%, Shock to 6. Now the feedback loop explodes — cascading defaults, currency in freefall, sovereign bond yields spike. This is what happened to Turkey 2018, Argentina 2001, and Asia 1997
  • Iran war extreme: Shock 8, Reserves 8, USD Debt 30%. Even with strong institutions, a severe enough shock (oil at $120 then collapsing, geopolitical chaos) can cause material stress. CAD could hit 1.50+, corporate refinancing gets painful
  • AI bubble pop + private credit + Iran (tail risk combo): Shock 9, Reserves 7 (slightly eroded by political uncertainty), USD Debt 35%. This is the "everything breaks" scenario. Even Canada's strong system would need emergency measures

Key takeaway

  • Canada is well-positioned: strong institutions, moderate USD exposure, Fed swap line access. But no country is immune to a large enough shock. The private credit market (~$1.7T US) is the current systemic risk that could trigger the next funding shock. Watch CLO spreads, HY bond yields, and bank earnings for early warning signs
Section 10

How the Forces Fit Together — The Canada–US Story

Everything above connects. Here’s the full chain applied to the CAD/USD in early 2026, and how each section maps to the current macro landscape.

1. BoC Sets Money Supply

BoC eased to 2.75% to support a sluggish economy. Fed held at 4.25% fighting sticky US inflation. 175bp gap = the root cause.

2. Capital Flows to the US

Canadian pension funds, banks, and investors chase higher US yields. Capital leaves Canada → demand for USD rises → CAD weakens.

3. CAD/USD Adjusts to 1.44

Interest rate parity: CAD must be weak enough that expected appreciation compensates for the rate gap. At 1.44, the math works out.

4. Trade & Balance Sheets React

Weak CAD boosts oil/lumber exports but raises import costs. BOP adjusts. USD debt gets pricier. The system finds a new equilibrium — or breaks.

Short run (Sections 2, 3, 7): Prices are sticky, interest rates diverge, and CAD overshoots — that's the 1.36 → 1.44 move. Long run (Section 4): PPP says lower Canadian inflation should gradually pull CAD back toward ~1.34. Policy (Sections 6, 8): Canada chose floating + free capital, so the BoC steers the ship — and fiscal policy leaks through the exchange rate. Crisis risk (Section 9): If private credit unwinds, AI bubble pops, or Iran escalates, the feedback loop can overwhelm the orderly adjustment.

The current macro shocks, mapped to sections

  • BoC vs Fed divergence (Sections 2, 3): THE dominant force. 175bp gap drives capital outflows and pins CAD at 1.44. Every BoC cut without a Fed cut widens the pressure
  • Iran war / oil shock (Sections 1, 5): Oil spikes help Canada's trade balance (Section 5) and shift USD supply right (Section 1). But geopolitical risk raises volatility, triggering risk-off flows that hurt CAD. Net effect: ambiguous, depends on whether oil stays high or spikes-then-crashes
  • AI bubble concerns (Sections 1, 9): If US tech valuations correct 30%+, Canadian pension funds (heavily exposed to US tech) take losses. Demand for USD drops (Section 1, slight CAD positive), but broader risk-off hits all non-US assets. Watch for contagion into private credit
  • Private credit unwind (Section 9): The ~$1.7T US private credit market has opacity, maturity mismatch, and leverage. If CLOs/BDCs face redemptions, funding dries up globally. Canadian banks have direct and indirect exposure. This is the tail risk that could trigger a crisis-loop scenario
  • US tariff threats (Sections 1, 5, 6): Tariffs directly cut Canada's exports (Section 5 trade balance), reducing USD inflows (Section 1). The weak CAD is the economy's automatic stabilizer (Section 6 — Mundell-Fleming). But tariff uncertainty alone is negative for investment
