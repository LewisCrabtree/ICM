Section 1

The Foreign Exchange Market

Before we can understand why the loonie moves, we need to see what an exchange rate actually is. CAD/USD is just a price — how many Canadian dollars it costs to buy one US dollar — set by supply and demand like any other market. Once you see the two curves, every section that follows is just a story about what shifts them.

Section 2

Money Supply → Interest Rates → Exchange Rates

Section 1 showed us that supply and demand set the exchange rate. But what drives those shifts in the short run? Central-bank policy is one major force. This section shifts from the flow-of-funds perspective in Section 1 to an asset-market approach: exchange rates are determined by stocks of deposits and expected returns, not just by trade flows per period. For teaching clarity, the section uses the classic money-market story: a more expansionary BoC stance lowers Canadian interest rates, which then affects CAD/USD through international capital flows. In practice the Bank implements policy through its rate corridor and expectations, so read the left panel as a simplified classroom representation rather than a literal operating manual.

  • This two-panel diagram is a useful short-run teaching benchmark. The BoC–Fed gap still matters for CAD, but real-world exchange rates also move with risk premia, commodity prices, and changing expectations
Section 3

Interest Rate Parity

Section 2 showed that higher US rates pull capital toward the US and weaken the loonie. But if that's true, why doesn't all capital flow to the US? There must be an equilibrium. Interest rate parity formalizes one benchmark answer: the market prices in expected CAD appreciation to compensate for the rate gap, helping us think about where today's exchange rate can settle. The chart deliberately holds commodity prices, terms of trade and currency risk premia constant.

UIP Benchmark Spot (E*)—
Rate Differential (BoC − Fed)—
Expected CAD Change (UIP)—

What the controls mean

  • BoC Rate (i): Canadian overnight rate at 2.25%. This is the vertical cyan line — the return you earn holding CAD deposits
  • Fed Rate (i*): US rate at 3.50–3.75%. Embedded in the green downward-sloping curve — the expected return from holding USD deposits
  • Third slider: under UIP it is expected future CAD/USD (Eᵉ); under CIP it becomes today's spot rate (S). The toggle changes the economic meaning of that input so the chart matches the parity condition being shown
  • Toggle UIP/CIP: UIP = risk-bearing bet on expected spot; CIP = risk-free forward contract priced off today's spot and the rate gap. Both use policy rates as proxies for matched-maturity deposit rates; in practice, 1-year term rates would differ due to the yield curve
  • What is held fixed here: commodity prices, terms of trade, and currency risk premia. Those omitted wedges can move spot CAD/USD materially even when rate differentials do not

How to read the chart

  • Under UIP, the cyan vertical line = Canadian interest rate = return on CAD deposits
  • Under UIP, the green downward curve = expected return on USD deposits (in CAD terms). It slopes down because: the weaker CAD is today (high E), the more CAD-appreciation you'd expect, so USD deposits look less attractive
  • Under CIP, the chart switches to a spot-versus-forward view. The forward point moves with today's spot and the rate gap, not with a separate expectations term

What this chart leaves out

  • Safe-haven USD demand, commodity prices, and Canada-specific risk premia can all push spot away from the parity benchmark for long periods
  • When you switch to CIP, the chart turns into a spot-versus-forward pricing view. CIP does not by itself deliver a separate structural theory of the spot rate

Empirical caveat: the forward premium puzzle

  • UIP’s empirical track record is poor. Since Fama (1984), decades of research have documented that higher-rate currencies tend to appreciate rather than depreciate as UIP predicts — the opposite of what the textbook says should happen. This is the “forward premium puzzle” and remains one of the most robust findings in international finance
  • For CAD/USD specifically, carrying USD at a higher interest rate has historically tended to deliver positive excess returns, not the zero the model assumes
  • The chart’s foreign-return curve uses a teaching linearization. Its slope is simplified for visual clarity — do not read it as a precise empirical estimate of how sensitive returns are to the exchange rate

  • See the current gap: BoC at 2.25%, Fed at 3.6%. The US pays about 1.3–1.4 percentage points more. In the chart's calibration, CAD/USD E* settles in the 1.38–1.39 area — CAD is weak NOW to offset the rate gap
  • What if BoC cuts more? Drag BoC rate from 2.25% → 1.5%. The cyan line shifts left → E* jumps higher → CAD weakens further. This is the parity-style logic if weak Canadian growth or tariff damage pushes the BoC lower
  • What if Fed cuts faster? Drag Fed rate from 3.6% → 2.5%. The green curve shifts left → E* drops → CAD strengthens. Faster Fed easing would remove part of the current rate disadvantage facing CAD

Key takeaway

  • The roughly 1.3–1.4 percentage point BoC–Fed gap helps explain why CAD is somewhat weak. In a parity framework, lower Canadian rates are partly offset by expected CAD appreciation, but actual spot rates also reflect trade shocks, energy shocks and risk premia that the textbook condition leaves out
Section 4

Purchasing Power Parity & the Long Run

Sections 2–3 explained short-run exchange rate moves through interest rates and capital flows. But where is CAD/USD headed over 5–10 years? One rough long-run anchor is inflation. This chart compounds the Canada–US inflation gap mechanically to show PPP direction, but real exchange rates also move with productivity, terms of trade, taxes and the tradable-versus-non-tradable mix.

Canada − US Inflation Gap—
Illustrative 10Y PPP Path—

What the controls mean

  • Canada CPI Inflation: a rough proxy for Canadian inflation. The 1.8% February 2026 reading was temporarily lowered by GST/HST tax effects, so treat it as a teaching default rather than a clean long-run signal. The cyan line in the chart
  • US CPI Inflation: the US headline rate (~2.4%). Using headline CPI keeps the comparison simple, but core measures and tradables prices may tell a different medium-run story. The green line
  • Starting CAD/USD: today's spot rate (~1.38–1.39). The chart projects forward 10 years from here

How to read the chart

  • The chart compounds the inflation gap into a mechanical nominal FX path. It is an illustration of PPP logic, not an estimated fair-value model
  • If Canada inflates faster: Canadian goods become relatively more expensive over time, so PPP-style reasoning points to a weaker CAD (higher E)
  • If US inflates faster: PPP-style reasoning points to some CAD appreciation over time, but the result should be read cautiously when the gap is driven by one-offs or by non-tradable prices
  • The "Illustrative 10Y" metric shows the chart's mechanical path, not a claim that CAD/USD should be at that level on schedule
  • This chart shows relative PPP — the rate-of-change version (inflation differentials drive FX changes). Absolute PPP (equal price levels across countries, via the law of one price) is a stronger and empirically harder condition that the chart does not test

Nominal vs. real exchange rate

  • This chart shows the nominal exchange rate (CAD per USD). Economists often care more about the real exchange rate, which adjusts for relative price levels: q = E × P*/P (spot rate × US prices / Canadian prices). The real rate captures purchasing-power-adjusted competitiveness
  • PPP is equivalent to saying the real exchange rate is constant over time. In practice, the real CAD/USD rate moves with productivity differences, terms of trade (especially energy prices for Canada), and the mix of tradable vs. non-tradable goods (the Balassa-Samuelson effect) — so it is not constant
  • When someone says “CAD is undervalued,” they usually mean the real rate has diverged from a long-run estimate. That divergence can persist for years and does not imply a near-term trading signal
  • Connecting rates and prices (Fisher equation): the real interest rate ≈ nominal rate − expected inflation. This links the money-market analysis in Sections 2–3 (nominal rates) to the inflation analysis here. When Canada's real rate is low relative to the US real rate, both interest-parity and PPP channels point the same way

🔬 Try This — Inflation scenarios

  • Current reality: leave defaults at Canada 1.8%, US 2.4%. Notice the gap is −0.6% — PPP gives CAD a mild appreciation bias over the long run. But part of Canada's low headline print reflects GST/HST tax effects, so that bias should be treated cautiously
  • Middle East energy shock spilling into Canada inflation: drag Canada inflation to 4.0%. If higher energy costs feed broadly into domestic prices and persist, PPP shifts toward a weaker CAD over time
  • Trade-war inflation on both sides: set both to 4.5%. When inflation rates move together, PPP gives little directional push — short-run interest rates and capital flows would still dominate
  • Back-to-target scenario: set both to 2.0%. When both economies converge near target, PPP adds very little trend pressure to CAD/USD

Key takeaway

  • PPP is one rough long-run anchor. It is most useful for direction over long horizons, not for near-term valuation calls, and it should sit alongside productivity and terms-of-trade stories rather than replacing them
Section 5

Balance of Payments

So far we've tracked exchange rates through money, interest rates, and prices. But there's a parallel accounting reality we've been ignoring: external balance. The current account includes goods, services, primary income and transfers, while the financial account records the offsetting financing. This section compresses those pieces into a teaching proxy so you can see the accounting logic and the J-curve without pretending that one slider measures the full external position.

What the controls mean

  • Goods & Services Balance (NX): the trade component of external balance. Negative = deficit (Canada imports more goods and services than it exports). This is only one piece of the current account
  • Rate Premium (BoC − Fed): the interest rate gap. Here it changes how comfortable the offsetting external financing looks; it does not move the current-account proxy directly
  • CAD Depreciation Shock: a sudden weakening of the loonie (e.g., from tariff news). This triggers the J-curve in the right panel

How to read the two panels

  • Left panel (bars): the cyan bar is a stylized current-account proxy tied to goods/services trade, and the purple bar is the offsetting financial flow. The note above the bars summarizes whether the rate gap makes that financing easier or less comfortable
  • Right panel (J-curve): after a CAD depreciation, imports get more expensive immediately (trade balance worsens), but over time Canadian exports may become more competitive and volumes adjust. The dip-then-recovery shape is the classic J-curve benchmark, not a guarantee on any fixed timeline

What this section simplifies

  • Exchange rate pass-through: the J-curve assumes depreciation eventually changes trade volumes. But the speed and completeness of pass-through from exchange rates to import/export prices matters enormously. Canada's pass-through to consumer goods is partial (~30–50% per BoC staff estimates), which limits how much and how quickly the J-curve adjustment actually works. As calibrated, the chart's long-run improvement is smaller than the initial worsening — consistent with partial pass-through keeping the recovery incomplete
  • Primary income and the NIIP: the current account includes not just trade in goods and services but also primary income (investment earnings flowing in and out) and transfers. Canada’s net international investment position (NIIP) is negative — foreign claims on Canada exceed Canadian claims abroad — so investment income tends to be a net outflow. This slider captures only the trade piece; the primary-income balance matters too, especially for how sustainable a given external position feels

🔬 Try This — Current events

  • US tariffs / trade-policy shock: drag Goods & Services Balance from −1.5 → −4.0. Exports fall, the external deficit widens, and Canada becomes more reliant on financing from abroad. With lower relative rates, that can put additional downward pressure on CAD
  • Middle East energy shock: drag Goods & Services Balance from −1.5 to +2.0. Canada is a net energy exporter, so higher oil and gas prices can improve trade income and reduce reliance on foreign financing. That is generally supportive for CAD — though persistent resource-driven appreciation can squeeze non-resource exporters (the Dutch Disease channel)
  • Rate gap and financing pressure: set Rate Premium to −1.5. The CA proxy stays tied to trade, but the financing note turns more adverse. Lower Canadian yields do not force one mechanical outcome, but they can make external financing less comfortable and increase exchange-rate sensitivity
  • J-curve after trade shock: set depreciation to 15%. Initially the trade balance worsens as import prices jump. Over time, if the Marshall-Lerner condition holds — the sum of export and import demand elasticities exceeds one — the trade balance can recover. The loonie's weakness is part of the adjustment mechanism, not a cure by itself

Key takeaway

  • The balance of payments is an accounting identity, not a behavioral law. The useful question is which part of the adjustment shows up through trade volumes, valuation, income flows or the exchange rate
Section 6

The Mundell-Fleming Model (IS-LM-BP)

We now have all the building blocks: money markets (Section 2), capital flows (Section 3), trade (Section 5). Mundell-Fleming ties them together into one diagram to answer the big policy question: when the BoC cuts rates or Ottawa spends billions, what happens to output? The answer depends on the exchange-rate regime. In the textbook floating-rate case with perfect capital mobility, monetary policy is fully effective while fiscal stimulus leaks entirely through the exchange rate. Real Canada is messier, so treat the diagram as comparative statics rather than a complete empirical ranking.

  • IS (cyan) = goods market equilibrium; LM (purple) = money market; BP (green) = external balance at world interest rate
  • Under FLOATING + high capital mobility: monetary policy is typically more powerful in the textbook case because depreciation can support net exports, while fiscal expansion is fully offset by appreciation
  • Under FIXED + high capital mobility: fiscal policy is typically more powerful in the textbook case because the central bank accommodates the peg, while monetary expansion is largely reversed to defend it
  • Why monetary policy fails under fixed rates: expansion → lower rates → capital outflows → currency pressure → CB sells reserves → money supply goes back
  • Why fiscal policy weakens under floating: stimulus → higher rates → capital inflows → appreciation → exports fall, fully offsetting the output gain
  • Canada caveat: fiscal policy can still matter materially, especially when households are balance-sheet constrained or shocks are sector-specific. The model is about relative short-run traction under restrictive assumptions
  • In Canada's current setting, tariffs are a negative IS shock and any BoC easing partly cushions that shock through a weaker CAD
  • The key textbook result: floating rates give central banks more room, while fixed rates give fiscal policy more traction
Section 7

Exchange Rate Overshooting

Mundell-Fleming told us which direction the exchange rate moves after a policy shock. But it didn't explain how much — and in practice, FX markets wildly overreact. When the BoC surprises with a cut, CAD doesn't gently drift to its new level — it plunges past it, then slowly recovers. Dornbusch (1976) explains why: goods prices are sticky, so the exchange rate must absorb the entire shock alone, temporarily overshooting to keep markets in equilibrium.

Illustrative CAD/USD Jump (overshoot)—
Stylized Long-Run CAD/USD Change—

What the controls mean

  • BoC Money Supply Increase (%): how big is the monetary expansion. 15% is a large shock (like cumulative QE). The bigger the increase, the bigger the overshoot
  • Price Stickiness (θ): how fast Canadian prices (wages, rent, groceries) adjust. Low θ = very sticky prices (slow adjustment) = MORE overshooting. High θ = prices adjust quickly = less overshoot
  • Implicit assumption — long-run monetary neutrality: the chart assumes that in the long run a 15% increase in the money supply raises both prices and the exchange rate by 15%, leaving real variables unchanged. This is a standard Dornbusch assumption

How to read the chart

  • The red spike is the initial CAD/USD jump — the currency overshoots past the dashed long-run line
  • The dashed horizontal shows the chart's stylized long-run level after prices adjust. It is an illustration of the overshooting mechanism, not a calibrated forecast path
  • The recovery path slopes downward as prices gradually adjust and the exchange rate comes back from its overshoot

🔬 Try This — Rate-cut and stress examples

  • A recent rate-cut style episode: In Oct 2024, BoC cut by 50bp in one shot. Set Money Supply to ~10% and θ to 0.5. The chart reproduces the kind of jump Dornbusch-style models are built to explain, but it is not an empirical fit to that episode
  • Why overshoot happens: Prices of Tim Hortons coffee, Toronto rent, and Ontario auto parts don't change overnight. So the exchange rate must do ALL the adjusting alone — and it overshoots to generate expected appreciation that makes investors willing to hold lower-yielding CAD assets
  • Stickier prices = bigger swings: drag θ from 0.5 → 0.15. The overshoot gets enormous. In any economy where prices adjust slowly relative to asset markets, the exchange rate must do more of the heavy lifting — amplifying volatility
  • Non-bank funding shock: if a major financial repricing hit and the BoC had to do emergency easing (Money Supply +30%), the stylized overshoot would be much larger. Treat that as a scenario illustration rather than a literal CAD target

Magnitudes caveat

  • At the default settings, the chart shows large overshoots (e.g., a 45% jump for a 15% money-supply increase). Part of this comes from implicitly setting the interest semi-elasticity of money demand (φ in Dornbusch's original notation) to 1, which amplifies the overshoot formula ΔE₀ = (1 + 1/θ) × ΔĒ. Empirical evidence for G7 currency overshooting of this magnitude from monetary shocks alone is essentially nonexistent (Rogoff 2002). Real-world G7 episodes typically involve single-digit percentage overshoots. The chart illustrates the mechanism of overshooting, not realistic G7 magnitudes — scale the intuition accordingly

Key takeaway

  • Exchange rate volatility is not necessarily irrational. In sticky-price models, CAD/USD can overshoot so investors expect appreciation while goods prices catch up. The mechanism is useful for intuition, even though real episodes also reflect risk premia, financial stress, and policy surprises
Section 8

The Impossible Trinity

Sections 6–7 assumed Canada floats its dollar. But why does Canada float? Is that the only option? The impossible trinity reveals the fundamental constraint: countries can have at most two of fixed rates, free capital, and independent monetary policy. Canada chose free capital + BoC autonomy, and the floating CAD is the price. This section shows what other countries chose — and why every regime is a tradeoff.

What the three corners mean

  • Fixed exchange rate: peg your currency to another (e.g., HKD pegged to USD). Gives stability but removes exchange rate as a shock absorber
  • Free capital mobility: let money flow across borders freely. Enables investment but makes you vulnerable to sudden stops
  • Independent monetary policy: set your own interest rates based on domestic conditions. Gives the CB power to fight recessions/inflation
  • The triangle says: pick any two, the third is impossible

The three scenarios

  • Fixed + Free Capital (sacrifice policy): Hong Kong, Eurozone (the ultimate fixed rate — a shared currency). The CB must match the anchor country's rates exactly. If the Fed hikes, HK must hike too, even if their economy is weak
  • Free Capital + Policy Autonomy (sacrifice fixed rate): Canada, US, UK, Japan. The BoC sets rates independently, but CAD/USD must float. This is Canada's choice — it's why CAD/USD trades around 1.38–1.39 instead of at a peg
  • Fixed + Policy Autonomy (sacrifice capital mobility): China historically, Malaysia 1998. You control capital flows with regulations. Beijing can set rates AND peg the yuan, but companies can't freely move money in/out

🔬 Try This — Why Canada floats under today's shocks

  • Click "Free Capital + Policy Autonomy" — this is Canada. The BoC can sit at 2.25% while the Fed sits at 3.50–3.75% because Canada accepts that CAD/USD floats around 1.38–1.39. If Canada pegged at 1.00, the BoC would be forced to match the Fed rate
  • Click "Fixed + Free Capital" — this is Hong Kong or EU members. When the Fed hiked aggressively in 2022-23, HK rates automatically followed even though their property market was crashing. No independent policy
  • Click "Fixed + Policy Autonomy" — this is China. The PBoC can ease while maintaining a stable yuan — but only because capital controls prevent money from fleeing. This is why Chinese investors struggle to buy Toronto real estate directly (they use workarounds)
  • Think about today's shocks: tariffs, energy volatility, or non-bank funding stress. A floating rate is part of Canada's adjustment mechanism — CAD can move, absorbing some of the shock. A peg would be harder to defend

Key takeaway

  • Every time CAD drops and someone asks "why doesn't Canada just peg to the USD?" — the answer is the trinity. Pegging would mean surrendering the BoC's ability to set rates for Canadian conditions. Canada chose policy freedom, and the volatile CAD is the cost
Section 9

Currency Crises & Feedback Loops

Everything up to now described orderly adjustment — markets finding equilibrium through arbitrage and policy. But what happens when the system breaks? For Canada, the relevant tail risk is less a classic reserve-defense crisis and more a funding-and-liquidity stress episode: market-based finance reprices sharply, USD funding becomes scarce, and parity conditions become poor guides. This section simulates the mechanics of that kind of breakdown.

What the controls mean

  • Funding Shock: how severe is the sudden repricing of risk. 0 = calm markets, 10 = full-blown panic. Think: private credit funds gating redemptions, wholesale funding stress, or tariffs colliding with a market selloff
  • Liquidity Backstop Credibility: how much markets trust BoC liquidity facilities, prudential backstops and Fed swap-line access to keep funding markets functioning. Canada scores high; a fragile emerging market would score much lower
  • Net FX Liability Exposure: a stylized share of liabilities or funding needs that remain exposed after hedging and rollovers. This is a teaching proxy, not a claim that 30% of all Canadian liabilities are unhedged USD debt

How the feedback loop works

  • Funding shock or sudden repricing → investors pull capital from Canada or demand more compensation → CAD weakens
  • CAD weakening → net FX liabilities and USD funding needs become more expensive in CAD terms → Canadian firms under stress → credit downgrades or forced hedging
  • Credit downgrades → more capital flight → CAD weakens further → vicious circle
  • The circle breaks when liquidity backstops are credible enough, hedges hold, the shock dissipates, or external help arrives

🔬 Try This — Stress test scenarios

  • Canada baseline (resilient): Shock 4, Backstop 8, FX Exposure 30%. The feedback loop is contained — CAD weakens moderately but does not spiral. This is Canada's stylized position: strong institutions buffer the shock
  • Non-bank repricing hits: drag Shock to 7 (private credit or wholesale funding markets tighten sharply). Keep Backstop at 8 and FX Exposure at 30%. Stress rises, but Canada's liquidity backstops keep it manageable. The BoC would lean on liquidity tools and swap lines if needed
  • Trade shock plus market stress: set Shock to 7, Backstop 8, FX Exposure 30%. A tariff hit to growth paired with tighter funding conditions can pressure CAD and refinancing conditions even without a classic crisis
  • What if Canada looked more like a fragile emerging market? Set Backstop to 3, FX Exposure to 65%, Shock to 6. Now the feedback loop explodes — the reserve-defense cases from Turkey 2018, Argentina 2001 or Asia 1997 become a better analogy than Canada
  • Combined stress: Shock 9, Backstop 7, FX Exposure 35%. Think tariffs, market repricing and energy volatility all arriving together. Even Canada's strong system would need emergency measures

Key takeaway

  • Canada is well-positioned: strong institutions, widespread hedging, domestic-currency liabilities and Fed swap-line access all matter. But no country is immune to a large enough shock. The live Canadian risk is better described as funding and market stress under trade uncertainty than as a base-case currency crisis
Section 10

Putting It All Together — Canada vs USA, March 2026

You've now seen every piece: how central banks move rates (2), how rates move exchange rates (3), where they're headed long-run (4), how trade adjusts (5), which policies work (6), why markets overreact (7), what constrains regime choice (8), and when it all breaks down (9). This final section uses those nine frameworks as a lens to explain why the main Canadian numbers sit where they do right now — and what scenarios the theory makes easiest to think about.

The question this section answers: You see the headlines — BoC at 2.25%, Fed at 3.50–3.75%, CAD/USD around 1.38–1.39, and 5Y GoC yields around 3.2%. Why are those numbers near where they are, and what do the models from Sections 1–9 suggest about the range of plausible next moves?

Part A — Why every number is where it is

🔗 The BoC–Fed gap: ~140bp at the Fed midpoint (Sections 2 & 8)

  • Section 2 says: in the textbook policy-stance diagram, a more expansionary BoC stance lowers Canadian interest rates. The right panel then shows the CAD return line shifting left → E rises → CAD weakens
  • Section 8 says: Canada can only do this because it chose the "free capital + policy autonomy" corner of the trinity. The floating CAD is the pressure valve that makes independent rate cuts possible. If Canada pegged to the USD, the BoC would be forced to hold at 3.50–3.75% alongside the Fed — imagine the pain for Canadian mortgage holders
  • Why ~140bp at the midpoint? The midpoint of the Fed's 3.50–3.75% range is about 1.4 percentage points above the 2.25% BoC rate. The March 18, 2026 Bank of Canada statement says Canadian growth has been softer than expected, housing remains weak, the labour market is soft, and unemployment rose to 6.7% in February. By contrast, the Fed kept rates higher while US inflation remained above target. Two different economies still imply two different policy settings

🔗 CAD/USD around 1.38–1.39 (Sections 1, 3 & 7)

  • Section 1 says: the roughly 1.4pp gap raises demand for USD (Canadians chasing higher yields) and reduces relative support for CAD assets. That helps explain why E sits near 1.39, though not by itself
  • Section 3 (UIP) says: in a simple parity benchmark, 1.39 is in the neighborhood of the rate where expected CAD appreciation can offset the rate gap you give up versus the Fed. That is a useful organizing framework, not a claim that spot FX is pinned down exactly by UIP every day
  • Section 7 says: overshooting is one plausible reason CAD moved more sharply than medium-run fundamentals alone would suggest. When the BoC surprised with the 50bp cut in Oct 2024, sticky prices likely made the exchange rate absorb more of the adjustment in the short run

🔗 Canada's trade balance (Section 5)

  • The BOP identity: the official external position is smaller than a simple "~2% of GDP deficit" shorthand suggests. Statistics Canada reported a seasonally adjusted current-account deficit of just C$0.7B in Q4 2025, although 2025 as a whole still showed a C$30.4B deficit. The teaching slider starts with a modest shortfall because the section is illustrating accounting logic, not reproducing one exact quarter
  • With the BoC at 2.25% and the Fed at 3.50–3.75%, Canada does not need to "solve" the external position through rates alone. But lower relative yields can make financing less comfortable and leave the exchange rate more sensitive to weaker terms of trade or softer investor appetite
  • The one clear offset is energy: the Bank of Canada noted in March that the Middle East war has pushed oil and natural gas prices sharply higher. That can support Canada's terms of trade, even while the same shock raises inflation and hurts global risk sentiment

🔗 Why Ottawa is not a mechanical fix (Section 6)

  • Mundell-Fleming's lesson: in the textbook floating-rate case with perfect capital mobility, fiscal stimulus is fully offset by appreciation — IS shifts right but the resulting capital inflows and appreciation shift it back. In practice, real-world capital mobility is high but not perfect, so some fiscal traction survives; still, the model's lesson is that much of the demand boost can leak into a stronger CAD and weaker net exports rather than translating into output
  • That does not mean fiscal policy is irrelevant. It means the BoC is usually the cleaner stabilization lever inside this model, while fiscal policy still matters through composition, timing, and household balance-sheet channels

Part B — What happens next? The models from Sections 2–9 do not deliver a single forecast. They help organize the most important conditional paths for Canadian interest rates and the exchange rate.

📉 Variable-Rate Mortgages

Variable mortgage pricing still moves mainly with the BoC rate. With the policy rate at 2.25%, variable borrowers are most exposed to the growth-versus-inflation tradeoff now facing the Bank: weaker activity and softer labour data argue for easier policy, while higher energy prices argue for caution.

📊 Fixed-Rate Mortgages (5Y)

Fixed mortgage pricing is tied more closely to Government of Canada bond yields than to the current overnight rate. With 5Y GoC yields around 3.2%, fixed borrowing costs are being shaped by expected future policy, term premia and geopolitical inflation risk rather than by the BoC alone.

💱 CAD/USD Exchange Rate

The most defensible baseline is range-based rather than point-based. The current rate gap, softer Canadian growth and lower Canadian inflation all lean in different directions, while trade policy and energy shocks can dominate in the short run. That makes CAD/USD around 1.38–1.39 easier to explain than any precise target for the next quarter.

⚠️ Risk Scenarios

The highest-profile scenarios in current 2026 reporting are not all the same kind of shock. The live set includes US tariff and trade-policy uncertainty, Middle East energy volatility, mortgage renewal and household-stress risk, non-bank or private-credit repricing, and a medium-term LNG export upside for Canada.

The exchange rate outlook, by time horizon

Forecasting caveat (Meese & Rogoff, 1983): structural exchange rate models — including the ones taught in this guide — cannot reliably outforecast a random walk at horizons under 1–2 years. The directional reasoning below is useful for understanding which forces matter, but should not be read as point or range forecasts with attached confidence.

  • Next 3–6 months (Sections 2, 3): the BoC–Fed gap modestly supports the USD, but the Bank of Canada now explicitly highlights both US tariff uncertainty and Middle East energy volatility, which can push CAD in opposite directions. Given the Meese-Rogoff result, specifying a narrow numerical range adds false precision — the models tell you which shocks to watch for, not where the rate will be in June
  • 12–24 months (Sections 3, 6): if US inflation cools enough for more Fed easing while the BoC mostly holds, CAD could firm somewhat. But that path is conditional on trade frictions not escalating and on growth in Canada not weakening enough to force renewed BoC cuts
  • 3–5 years (Section 4 — PPP): lower Canadian inflation than US inflation still points to a mild long-run appreciation bias for CAD in PPP-style reasoning. But PPP is a slow anchor, not a timing tool, and the current Canada print needs caution because headline CPI was affected by tax-related distortions

The rate outlook, by instrument

  • BoC overnight rate: currently 2.25%. The Bank's March statement says risks to growth are tilted to the downside, but inflation risks have risen because of higher energy prices. That makes the near-term path more plausibly "hold with easing bias" than a clean march lower
  • 5Y GoC bond yield: currently around 3.2% based on Bank of Canada benchmark data. It reflects expected short rates, term premium and risk compensation. That means fixed borrowing costs can stay elevated even if the BoC does not hike again
  • Variable mortgage rates: these still reset most directly with prime and the policy rate, but lender discounts move with credit conditions. In a weak-growth scenario they would usually adjust down faster than fixed rates; in an energy-driven inflation scare they may not
  • Fixed 5Y mortgage rates: these depend more on bond markets than on the next BoC meeting. A softer economy could pull them down somewhat, but a higher term premium or renewed inflation concern could keep them relatively sticky

Mortgage pricing through the lens of the models

  • Variable mortgage rates move mainly with the BoC overnight rate (Section 2’s channel). Fixed mortgage rates move mainly with bond yields, term premia and inflation expectations (Section 4’s domain). These are different exposures to different shocks
  • The models taught here clarify which macroeconomic forces affect which type of rate. They do not deliver a mortgage recommendation — that depends on personal risk tolerance, cash-flow variability, prepayment flexibility and other factors outside the scope of an international finance framework

Most pressing scenarios for Canadians now

  • 1. US tariffs and trade-policy uncertainty (Sections 1, 5, 6): this is the scenario most clearly emphasized in current Bank of Canada language and in current Canada-focused news searches. The key channel is weaker exports, softer business investment and added downside pressure on Canadian growth
  • 2. Middle East war and energy-market volatility (Sections 1, 5): the Bank of Canada now explicitly flags the conflict as a source of higher oil and gas prices, tighter financial conditions and more inflation risk. For Canada this is a mixed shock: better terms of trade on one side, more global risk aversion and higher gasoline prices on the other
  • 3. Mortgage renewal strain and household vulnerability (Sections 2, 6, 9): Bank of Canada financial-stability material continues to emphasize debt-service pressure, high loan-to-income borrowers and household stress indicators. This is less dramatic than a currency crisis, but probably more relevant to everyday Canadian macro risk
  • 4. Non-bank finance and private-credit repricing (Sections 7, 9): current searches and Bank of Canada financial-stability work still point to severe-repricing risk in markets and non-bank credit. For Canada this matters mainly through funding conditions, pension exposure and broader risk sentiment, not because a crisis is the base case
  • 5. LNG and export diversification upside (Sections 1, 5, 8): search results increasingly point to Canada's LNG build-out as a medium-term strategic story, especially for Asia and potentially Europe. This is better treated as an upside structural scenario for trade and investment than as an immediate March 2026 driver of CAD

The bottom line: The BoC–Fed gap still matters, but it no longer explains everything by itself. In March 2026 the live Canadian macro story is a combination of softer domestic activity, trade-policy uncertainty, higher energy prices and household financial strain. The models in this guide are frameworks for organizing thinking about these forces — not forecasting tools and not financial advice.
